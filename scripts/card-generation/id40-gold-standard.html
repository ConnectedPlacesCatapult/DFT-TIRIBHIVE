Key insight

SuDS reduced river discharge from 69.6 to 9.2 litres/sec in a 1-in-100-year event. Even with a 30% climate change uplift applied, discharge remains below 12.1 litres/sec. The scheme is now the default approach for Sheffield city centre regeneration and has inspired an £80m Severn Trent SuDS project in Mansfield.

Main applications
  • Urban transport corridors where road runoff contributes to downstream flooding of rail or tram infrastructure
  • Rail and tram lines following river valleys — scheme directly protects Sheffield rail and tram network downstream
  • Local authority highway flood management programmes operating within existing maintenance budgets
  • City centre regeneration projects where climate adaptation can be bundled with place-making investment
  • Any infrastructure where grey surfacing has been identified as a primary contributor to catchment runoff volumes
Key takeaways
  • Nature-based SuDS can outperform grey flood infrastructure at lower long-term cost — avoided maintenance costs from removing bituminous surfacing are ringfenced for scheme upkeep
  • Phased delivery reduces capital risk — Phase 1 at £3.6m demonstrated viability before the Phase 2 (£6.3m) commitment was made
  • Cross-sector impact is underappreciated — this roads project also protects rail, tram, and 1,275 residential properties downstream
  • University partnership (Sheffield) provided design credibility and community consultation capacity the council lacked internally
  • Specialist horticultural knowledge is essential for long-term success — schemes that focused only on engineering without plant maintenance have failed elsewhere

Implementation notes

Specialist horticultural skills required for plant selection and long-term maintenance — not standard council highway capacity. Only half the highway was re-profiled due to service depth constraints. Initial business resistance to a 'wild landscape' aesthetic required active stakeholder engagement before buy-in was achieved. Native British flora is not always appropriate in urban soil substrates — specialist ecological assessment needed.

Implementation challenges
  • Salt runoff from de-icing activities affects plant health — ongoing soil and plant monitoring required
  • Biodiversity net gain requirements can conflict with optimal plant species choices in urban engineered substrates
  • Local authority procurement and maintenance capability is a barrier to wider replication without knowledge transfer
Lessons learned
  • Multiple smaller green spaces distributed across an urban area can match the flood mitigation impact of one large centralised scheme
  • Quality of planting design and ongoing maintenance is as important as the engineering works — schemes focused only on infrastructure have failed when plants were neglected
  • Academic partnership provided design credibility, feasibility capacity, and community consultation support that the council could not resource internally
Innovation opportunity

Automated stormwater planters connected to weather forecast data — pre-emptively drain storage before storm events, then refill during heavy rainfall. Trialled in a similar project in Hull. Would remove the need for manual operation during storm events.
